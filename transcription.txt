    \begin{abstract}
        The current encryption of most bank data uses the \textsc{Rsa} cipher, invented in 1977 by Ronald \textsc{Rivest}, Adi \textsc{Shamir} and Leonard \textsc{Adleman}. The difficulty of deciphering lies in the difficulty of factoring integers, especially when the integers chosen are very large. Factorisation algorithms have already been designed and implemented on conventional computers, but in practice they take too long to calculate in a human lifetime.
        
        In 1994, the mathematician Peter \textsc{Shor} proposed an algorithm for factoring integers using the recent technology of quantum bits, also known as q-bits. This algorithm is not widely used at present because of its cost in q-bits, which, although modest, is too high for current resources. However, it is far more efficient than previous algorithms, and makes encryption much more fragile in the long term.
        
        This article explains how this algorithm works. It will present elementary notions of quantum computation, the quantum fourier transform and the quantum discrete logarithm. In the appendices, you will find the arithmetic details of the \textsc{Rsa} encryption as well as various mathematical results used.
    \end{abstract}
